Key Attractions and Activities

County Meath sits in the historic Boyne Valley, where Neolithic tombs and Norman castles share the landscape with lively towns and lush countryside. If you have extra time before or after the wedding, here are some ideas.



  • Exploring ancient history I

    The Hill of Tara
    • The Hill of Tara

    County Meath is home to the legendary Hill of Tara, once the sacred seat of the High Kings. We invite you to take in the breathtaking views and ancient atmosphere that have made this landscape a symbol of Irish sovereignty and spirit for over 5,000 years.

    The Hill of Tara is open year-round with free access to the grounds, making it a perfect spot for a morning stroll.. Because the terrain is made up of grassy, uneven earthworks, we recommend bringing sturdy walking shoes if you plan to explore the summit. On a clear day, you can see as many as half the counties in Ireland.

    For more information, visit https://heritageireland.ie/places-to-visit/hill-of-tara/

  • Exploring ancient history II

    Brú na Bóinne
    • Brú na Bóinne

    Before the Pyramids emerged from the sand or Stonehenge stood tall, this place was home to a sophisticated wealthy society intent on leaving their mark on Ireland’s landscape – and oh, what a mark they made. These talented astronomers, geologists, engineers and builders organised large numbers of people to build huge monuments, leaving an enduring legacy that still astonishes.

    Sitting within the inland island known as the Bend of the Boyne, the UNESCO World Heritage Site of Brú na Bóinne safeguards the largest collection of megalithic art in Europe, dotted across over 90 Neolithic monuments. Best known of these are the vale’s three most spectacular sites: Newgrange, Knowth and Dowth. Each holds its own unique story – but it’s the confluence of all three in the heart of Ireland’s richest archaeological landscape that makes a trip here simply unparalleled.

    Access to Newgrange and Knowth is through the Brú na Bóinne Visitor Centre only. Here you’ll find a state-of-the-art interpretive centre where you can learn more about Neolithic life and enjoy guided tours of these extraordinary monuments. Online booking is recommended.

    For more information, visit https://www.ireland.com/en-gb/things-to-do/attractions/bru-na-boinne/

  • Castles

    Trim slane-castle-1
    • Trim Castle

    Trim Castle, located on the south bank of the River Boyne in County Meath, stands as the largest and most impressive Anglo-Norman castle in Ireland. Its construction began around 1172 under Hugh de Lacy and continued for over 30 years, eventually serving as the powerful administrative capital for the Lordship of Meath. The site's strategic elevated position was chosen to overlook a critical fording point on the river, symbolizing Norman dominance over the surrounding landscape.

    The fortress is best known for its unique 20-sided cruciform keep, a three-storey masterpiece of 12th-century experimental military architecture with walls up to three metres thick. Surrounding this central tower is an expansive three-acre enclosure protected by massive curtain walls and a series of defensive towers and gatehouses. Despite periods of decline starting in the 16th century, much of its original medieval fabric remains remarkably intact, as the castle was never modernised into a residential home in later centuries.

    In modern times, Trim Castle achieved global fame as a primary filming location for the 1995 Academy Award-winning epic Braveheart, where its imposing walls stood in for the city of York and the Tower of London.

    For more information, visit https://www.ireland.com/en-gb/things-to-do/attractions/trim-castle/.

  • Exploring less ancient history

    Battle of the Boyne
    • Battle of the Boyne

    The Battle of the Boyne Visitor Centre in Oldbridge, County Meath gives an in-depth insight into the battle between King William III and his father-in-law King James II in 1690. Explore the colourful tale of the Battle of the Boyne through detailed displays and historical reenactments.

    Visit The Battle of the Boyne Visitor Centre in the recently restored 18th century Oldbridge House on the banks of the River Boyne. Learn the story of one of the most important battles in Irish history. See models of the battle brought to life through narration and lasers showing the cunning guile of the victors. Plan your visit around the historical reenactments where guides dress up in replica costumes and recreate the famous battle.

    Stroll around the gardens after visiting the house. Large walls, covered in flowers surround a natural amphitheatre, while the nearby parkland and Townley Hall Forest are great places for a walk with friends and family. After, relax in the modern coffee shop and look out at the flowers in the gardens.

    For more information, visit https://www.ireland.com/things-to-do/attractions/battle-of-the-boyne/.

  • Scenic valley full of history

    Glendalough, Wicklow
    • Glendalough, Wicklow

    In a stunning glaciated valley in County Wicklow, in the sixth century, one of Ireland’s most revered saints founded a monastery. The foundation of St Kevin at Glendalough became one of the most famous religious centres in Europe.

    Wicklow Mountains National Park is a natural oasis just a stone’s throw from Dublin, making it an ideal getaway from Ireland’s capital. At the south end of the park lies the spellbinding settlement called Glendalough. Here you’ll find one of the most important ancient monastic sites in Ireland.

    For more information, visit https://www.ireland.com/en-us/things-to-do/attractions/glendalough/

  • Capital city

    Dublin
    • Dublin

    Approximately one hour way, Dublin's fair city awaits. It is a lively, walkable city full of history and culture.

    Suggestions if you are arriving early or staying a bit longer. Spend an afternoon visiting the world renowned Guinness and Jameson factory. Also, feel free to explore Trinity College, the book of kells, Dublin Castle, The General Post Office, Kilmanhaim Jail.

    For more information, visit https://www.visitdublin.com/

  • Coastal Town

    Skerries
    • Skerries

    Located just 30km north of Dublin’s city centre, Skerries is a picturesque coastal town that perfectly blends a vibrant fishing heritage with a relaxed seaside atmosphere. Named after the Norse word skere (meaning "the rocks"), this former Viking landing spot remains remarkably unspoilt, offering visitors a refreshing alternative to the bustling capital. With its unique west-facing harbour—a rare find on Ireland’s east coast—it is the ideal place to enjoy a sunset over the water while watching local trawlers bring in the day’s catch of famous Dublin Bay prawns.

    The town’s skyline is defined by the historic Skerries Mills, a unique heritage site featuring two restored windmills and a watermill that date back to the 12th century. Beyond the mills, the landscape is dotted with Napoleonic-era Martello towers and the five Skerries Islands, which host some of Europe’s most significant bird colonies. For those looking to explore, the Skerries Coastal Walk loops around the headland of Red Island, connecting the rugged South Strand with the calmer North Beach, perfect for a leisurely stroll or a brave dip in the Irish Sea.

    After a day of exploring, Skerries truly shines through its award-winning culinary scene, centered primarily around the lively harbour front. You can indulge in fresh seafood at local favourites like Blue Bar or Stoop Your Head..

    For more information, visit https://www.visitdublin.com/guides/things-to-do-skerries

  • Scenic Coastal Village

    Howth
    • Howth

    Howth is a captivating peninsula that serves as the perfect introduction to Ireland’s coastal beauty. Historically a Viking settlement, this fishing village seamlessly blends its rugged maritime heritage with a refined culinary scene. Visitors are immediately greeted by the sight of colorful trawlers in the harbour and the playful grey seals that frequent the piers, making it an essential stop for anyone wanting to experience the authentic "salt-of-the-earth" Irish atmosphere without traveling far from the capital.

    For those who love the outdoors, the Howth Cliff Walk offers one of the most breathtaking coastal hikes in the country. The trail winds along the dramatic edges of the peninsula, providing panoramic views of the Irish Sea, the uninhabited bird sanctuary of Ireland’s Eye, and the iconic Baily Lighthouse.

    After working up an appetite on the cliffs, the village’s legendary seafood awaits. Howth is widely considered the seafood capital of the Dublin coast, with a row of award-winning restaurants along the West Pier, such as Aqua and The Oar House. From world-class fish and chips enjoyed on the pier to elegant tapas featuring the day's local catch, the dining experience here is as fresh as it gets. Ending a day with a pint in a traditional pub like The Abbey Tavern, while listening to live folk music, encapsulates the quintessential charm of an Irish seaside escape.

    For more information, visit https://www.discoverireland.ie/dublin/howth-cliff-path-loop/



Activities

Walk the Boyne Valley Camino

The 25km walk forms a figure of eight looped walk via Townley Hall Woods on the way out to Mellifont Abbey, through Tullyallen Village, down King William’s Glen via Belnumber Wood, across the beautiful Boyne at the Obelisk and back into Drogheda via Oldbridge along the Boyneside Trail and ramparts. The Boyne Valley Camino (25km) is part of the Celtic Camino from Ireland, then walk the rest of the Celtic Camino from A Coruña to Santiago de Compostela (75km) in Spain. This 100km Celtic Camino entitles walkers to a Compostela in Santiago.

Visit Emerald Park

Emerald Park Opening Hours provides the most up-to-date information on when Ireland’s only theme park and zoo is open for visitors, with varied times throughout the year so you can plan your family visit accordingly. The park features thrilling rides, animal experiences, shows, and other attractions set in the heart of the Boyne Valley.

Play Golf

Discover three outstanding golf experiences in Ireland: L & B Golf Club (Laytown & Bettystown Links) , a classic links course set along the Irish Sea offering undulating fairways, coastal winds, and a traditional seaside test steeped in over a century of history; Bellewstown Golf Club , a beautifully maintained parkland course in East Meath with stunning views over the Boyne Valley and mature trees framing its fairways; and Royal Tara Golf Club , a highly regarded parkland venue near Navan that has hosted championship events and offers a scenic, rewarding challenge in Ireland’s lush countryside.

Beach Walks

Laytown Beach is a scenic sandy stretch on County Meath’s east coast, popular for peaceful seaside walks, fresh sea air, and wide views along the shoreline. It’s also well known for the annual Laytown Races—an iconic event where horses race directly on the firm sand—making the beach a memorable stop for both relaxation and local tradition.

Food and Drink

Pubs

The Snail Box

The Snail Box has the largest display of baseball caps in Europe - currently over 7000 and counting. The collection is always growing and you can add to it. They have caps from Ashbourne to Abu-Dhabi and from Bohermeen to Baghdad.

J O'Connell Pub

J'Connell's can be found on the Hill of Skyrne. It was made famous when Guinness used it for their Christmas advert. It popularly known as 'Mrs O's' and has practically stayed the same for about 100 years. Its located opposite a national monument called Skreen Church which is also worth a visit.

Foxs pub

Fox's has been pulling pints on the hill of Skryne for almost 50 years. Family-run traditional pub located on picturesque hill of Skryne with lovely views of the countryside. A warm welcome and a great pint of Guinness awaits you. Well known for the great traditional music on a Wednesday if you are arriving early.

Matt the Thresher (Julianstown)

A warm welcome awaits you at Matt the Thresher, Julianstown. Nestled in the heart of County Meath, our award-winning gastro bar and restaurant offers a relaxed yet refined dining experience that celebrates the best of land and sea.

Restaurants

Conyngham Arms

The Conyngham Arms is a well-known restaurant in Slane offering classic Irish hospitality in a relaxed and welcoming setting. It’s popular for its hearty dishes, traditional favourites, and comfortable atmosphere, making it a reliable choice for casual dining in the town.

Bellinter

Bellinter House Restaurant, located just outside Navan, provides an elegant dining experience set within a beautiful Georgian estate. Known for its refined cuisine and tranquil surroundings, it’s an ideal spot for special occasions, romantic dinners, or a leisurely meal in the countryside.

Eatzen

Eatzen is a Chinese restaurant in Ashbourne, loved for its fresh ingredients, creative menu, and friendly vibe. It’s a great option for brunch, lunch, or relaxed evening dining, appealing to those looking for contemporary flavours and a laid-back atmosphere.

Cinnamon Garden

Cinnamon Garden is a popular Indian restaurant in Ashbourne, known for its rich flavours, aromatic spices, and wide range of traditional dishes. With attentive service and a warm ambience, it’s a favourite choice for sit-down dining.
